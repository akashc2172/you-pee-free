abstract
Reﬂux nephropathy and vesicoureteral reﬂux are two of the most important abnormalities in the upper
urinary system in which toxins and bacteria from the bladder infect the ureter and the kidney and
initiate renal scar formation. A quantitative analysis that characterizes urine ﬂow will further help our
understanding of the ureter and also assist in the design of ﬂow aided devices such as valves and stents
to correct reﬂux situations. Here, A numerical simulation with ﬂuid–structure interactions (FSI) using
arbitrary Lagrangian–Eulerian (ALE) formulation and adaptive mesh procedure was introduced and
solved to perform ureteral ﬂow analysis. Incompressible Navier–Stokes equations were utilized as the
governing equations of ﬂuid domain. Ureteral in-vivo morphometric data during peristalsis were used
to construct the presented model. A nonlinear material model was used to exhibit ureteral wall
mechanical properties. Direct coupling method was used to solve the solid, ﬂuid and interface
equations simultaneously. Results showed that recirculation regions formed against the jet ﬂow,
neighboring the bolus peak. Through wave propagation, separation occurred behind the moving bolus
on the wall and ureteropelvic reﬂux began from that location and extended upstream to the ureteral
inlet. The maximum luminal pressure consistently occurred behind the urine bolus during peristalsis.
The measured magnitude of maximum volumetric ﬂow rate resulted from isolated bolus transportation
was 0.92 ml/min. In conclusion; due to presence of ﬂuid inertial forces during peristalsis, the function
of ureteropelvic junction in prevention of reﬂux is signiﬁcant, especially at the beginning of peristaltic
wave propagation. Moreover, modeling of ureteral function using imaging data will be valuable and it
may help physicians to diagnose and cure the abnormalities.

1. Introduction
Under customary ﬂow conditions, the ureter transports urine
in isolated boluses ( Mastrict and Tauecchio, 1984 ). Each one bolus
of urine is pushed by a cell to cell moving contraction of the
ureteral compliant wall. The mechanism of ureteral peristalsis has
been a major topic for investigation from the early beginnings
of peristaltic research ( Fung, 1971 ). Numerical analysis of the
peristaltic ﬂow in the ureter attempts to advance our knowledge
of the reﬂux phenomenon and other ureteral abnormalities. From
a clinical point of view, vesicoureteral reﬂux and reﬂux nephro-
pathy are two major abnormalities in the upper urinary tract.
Reﬂux nephropathy usually happens in cases of pyelorenal back-
ﬂow with infected urine. Bacteria rise from the bladder to the
kidney, and maximal bladder pressures and different probable
conditions of peristalsis in the ureter inﬂuence it ( Jorgensen,
1986). Renal scar formation is a common result of reﬂux
nephropathy. Moreover, reﬂux may infect and hurt kidney func-
tion ( Bykova and Regirer, 1999 ) thus resulting in dialysis or in
critical cases kidney transplantation ( Eccles, 1998 ).
Relevant investigations performed in the ﬁeld of biomedicine up
till now have been restricted to (1) Urine volume transported via
peristalsis, (2) Propagation rate of peristaltic wave (Saeki et al., 1985),
(3) Morphometry of the ureter ( Zelenko et al., 2004 ), (4) Dynamic
changes of ureteral lumen during peristalsis ( Woodburne and
Lapides, 1972), (5) Response of the ureteral muscular tissue to
drugs (Kontani et al., 1993 ; Mahoney et al., 2006 ), and (6) Various
ﬂow patterns taking place in the ureter of different mammalians
(Ohlson, 1989a,b; Fung, 1993). Studies in the ﬁeld of biomechanics
have been concentrated on understanding the mechanical properties
of the ureteral wall ( Hansen and Gregersen, 1999 ; Yin and Fung,
1971) and mathematical analyses of peristaltic ﬂow in the urinary
system (Grifﬁths and Notschaele, 1983; Gintz et al., 2001; Vogel et al.,
2004; Vahidi and Fatouraee, 2007 ; Jime´nez-Lozano, 2009; Vahidi
et al., 2011).
We formerly simulated a two-dimensional ureteral peristaltic
activity that assumed a solid wall moving along the longitudinal
direction between two rigid plates, ( Vahidi and Fatouraee, 2007 ).

The principal limitation of that study was that the cell to cell
stimulation of the ureteral wall during peristalsis, which takes
place under physiological conditions of ureteral peristalsis, was
not considered. We also presented an axisymmetric model using
ureteral real data removing this shortcoming ( Vahidi et al., 2011 ).
There, we investigated the effects of different mechanical para-
meters on ureteral ﬂow. However, the open-tube regime of ﬂow
in the ureter during peristalsis were simulated alone and the
lower ﬂow rate regime of isolated bolus transportation in the
ureter was not considered which occurs most commonly through
peristalsis in real physiological conditions of human ureter
(Ohlson, 1989b ). Therefore, in order to analyze this ﬂow regime,
an axisymmetric nonlinear FSI model is constructed using real
ureteral data of nonlinear wall mechanical properties, peristaltic
wave velocity and ureteral luminal morphometric data during
peristalsis. The present study is carried out to understand more
precisely this transport mechanism compared to the recent
previous studies (e.g., Gintz et al., 2001 ; Vogel et al., 2004 ;
Jime´ nez-Lozano, 2009 ) that is performed through computational
approaches using ﬂuid–structure interactions method.
In this paper, we intend to investigate ureteral peristaltic
activity, taking into consideration ureteral wall compliance and
its cell to cell stimulation, in order to study urine ﬂow and
pressure during peristalsis and detect temporal incidence of
reﬂux phenomenon.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Geometry of the numerical model
The main goal of this study is to construct a mathematical model
which aims to mechanically simulate the sophisticated nature of
ureteral peristalsis. In order to do that, in-vivo morphometric data of
ureteral lumen during peristalsis ( Woodburne and Lapides, 1972 )
were utilized. Geometry of the presented numerical model is shown
in Fig. 1 . This model illustrates the initial condition of the ureter
before initiation of wall stimulation. The whole length of an adult
ureter is about 30 cm that was as sumed in the presented model
(Fig. 1 (a)) and each urine bolus ranges from 3 to 12 cm in length
(Jime´ nez-Lozano, 2009). The initial bolus of urine collected at the
proximal portion of the ureter with the length of 6 cm can be seen in
Fig. 1(b). The proportion between luminal areas at the positions of
bolus peak and ureteral collapsed regions could simply be calculated
by dividing the two areas shown in Fig. 1(b).

2.3.2. Urine model
Transient ﬂow of urine in an axisymmetric model of ureter
was assumed. The ﬂuid ﬂow was assumed to be laminar,
Newtonian, viscous and incompressible with Density ( r) and
viscosity ( m0) of 1000 kg/m 3 and 1.3 cP ( Bykova and Regirer,
2005), respectively. At the ureteral inlet, normal traction of
100 Pa was imposed that is in the range of the constant pressure
level in the ureter ( Jime´ nez-Lozano, 2009). Continuity equation of
conservation of mass (Eq. (3)) and the incompressible Navier–
Stokes equations (Eq. (4)) were used as the governing equations
for the ﬂuid.
2.4. Contact analysis
Wall stimulation of the ureter was performed using a rigid
contact surface as it is shown in Fig. 1 &. The rigid contact surface
is initially in contact with the ureteral wall and after elapsing
some time steps of transient analysis (that results in a steady ﬂow
between ureteral inlet and outlet due to the applied pressure
difference) at t¼1 s, this rigid contact surface moves in the
longitudinal direction with an average physiological velocity of
ureteral peristalsis of 2 cm/s. This velocity magnitude was utilized
in our simulation because the range of physiological velocity of
peristalsis is reported to vary from 2 to 6 cm/s ( Jime´ nez-Lozano,
2009). The peristalsis time was assumed 15 s in our study because
it takes 15 s for the peristaltic wave to cover whole length of the
ureter (30 cm) with the constant velocity of 2 cm/s. To model the
interaction of boundary of rigid surface and the ureteral outer
wall, contact condition was setup between the moving rigid
surface and outer boundary of the ureteral wall. Frictionless
contact analysis was performed between the rigid surface and
the ureteral wall boundaries which is less costly to use comparing
to frictional analysis if the contact surfaces are smooth. In our
study, the constraint function method which is more effective
than the segment method in most frictionless contact problems
(Bathe, 2008a ) was used as the contact solution algorithm. Large
contact displacement formulation was utilized. Penetrations
between the rigid surface and the wall elements were not allowed
by the contact algorithm. Once penetration was detected, the
overlapping elements were returned to their positions at the
previous time step. The rigid surface was meshed assuming linear
contact segments including 2-node per segment. Tolerance for
nodal coincidence checking for the rigid contact surface nodes
was set to 10

In the direct coupling method which was used in the pre-
sented solution, ﬂuid stress and solid displacement have also
been relaxed, the same as in the iterative coupling method, using
a stress relaxation factor lt and a displacement relaxation factor
ld, respectively. These factors have been proven helpful for many
difﬁcult problems ( Bathe, 2008b). Stress and displacement relaxa-
tion factors were assumed 0.9 and 1, respectively.
Solution process is in a way that the linearized system is
solved at each iteration k and the succeeding solutions for the
solid and ﬂuid are updated. Stress or displacement Residuals are
subsequently calculated and compared against speciﬁed toler-
ances. If the solutions have not yet converged, the procedure
iterates to k þ1 and repeats the referred linearization route. When
the solutions have converged, the method continues to the next
time step. In brief, four common computational steps exist in the
direct coupling method utilized; ﬁrstly, to assemble the solid and
ﬂuid equations independently into a distinct ﬂuid and distinct
solid model; secondly, to assemble the solid matrix, ﬂuid matrix,
and the coupling matrices into one coupled matrix system;
thirdly, to solve the linearized equation of the coupled system
and to bring up to date the solution; and lastly, to calculate and
compare the residuals with the determined tolerance. In case the
solution diverged, the process was restarted from the initial step
(Zhang and Bathe, 2001 ; Bathe and Zhang, 2004 ).
The model was solved numerically using the commercial
package ADINA-FSI (Adina R&D Inc., Watertown MA, USA). 10
time steps with the magnitude of 0.1 s were considered for the
non-peristaltic phase, before beginning of wall stimulation. Dur-
ing this period of time, a steady ﬂow occurred in the ureter due to
the applied pressure difference between its inlet and outlet. We
assumed 1500 time steps with the magnitude of 0.01 s during
peristalsis.

Mesh convergence analysis examining ﬂow parameters and
stress/displacement with coarser and ﬁner meshes were checked
that the numerical results are independent of mesh density. After
analysis of mesh size independence of solution, ﬂuid domain was
meshed with 40,000 triangular 2-D Fluid Axisymmetric type
elements which contain 3-nodes. 3000 Quadrilateral 2-D Solid
Axisymmetric type elements having 9-nodes were used for the
solid domain. The initial mesh of the ﬂuid and structure at the
ureteral inlet is shown in Fig. 1(c). The ﬂuid and the structure cells
were all deﬁned as axisymmetric, so that they supposedly
extended circumferentially by one radian. Although the imple-
mented FSI procedure does not require that the ﬂuid grid match
the structure grid, this arrangement guaranteed that ﬂuid cells
remained adjoining with structure cells in the direction of the
straight lines between nodes at the ﬂuid/structure interaction
boundaries. To preserve the contiguity of the combined mesh,
ﬂuid–structure interaction boundaries were determined in both
the solid and ﬂuid domains.
3. Results
Experimental data of ureteral wall properties from (Yin and Fung,
1971) were ﬁtted by this material model as illustrated in Fig. 2.
Urine pressure proﬁle at a point in peristalsis is shown in
Fig. 3 . Analysis of urine pressure during peristalsis showed that
the maximum pressure in the ureter during peristalsis consis-
tently occurred behind the moving bolus which is much higher
than the magnitude of initial constant pressure applied to the
ureteral inlet.
The results indicated that urine velocity is maximum inside
the moving bolus, neighboring the centerline of the ureter which
leads to a high velocity jet ﬂow there, as it can be seen in Fig. 4 a.
Also, recirculation regions formed against the jet ﬂow, neighbor-
ing the bolus peak. The urine velocity vector plot behind the
moving bolus ( Fig. 4b) shows that there are consistent stagnation
points from which urine retrogrades from the ureter into the

4. Discussion
In this study, a computational simulation was proposed to
simulate ureteral peristalsis in which intraluminal geometrical
dynamic changes of the ureter through the wave propagation is more
realistic in comparison with previously published investigations (e.g.,
Carew and Pedley, 1997; Gintz et al., 2001 ; Jime´nez-Lozano, 2009).
Through this simulation, dynamic interactions between urine and the
ureteral wall are explored. It is worth noting that the complicated
nature of ureteral physiological muscular changes during peristalsis
that occur in both circumferential and longitudinal directions were
not considered here which need much more thoughtfulness and care.
Grifﬁths et al. (1987) reported that peristalsis is not strictly
periodic in the ureter. We used a ﬁnite length tube with
constrained outlet condition and in order to make peristalsis, a
moving contraction wave was implemented. These considerations
removed the necessity for using simplifying assumptions such as
sinusoidal contraction waves and periodic boundary conditions
(as were assumed in the investigations of Lykoudis and Roos
(1970), Manton (1975) , Shapiro et al. (1969) , Takabatake et al.
(1988), Xiao and Damodaran (2002) , Yin and Fung (1969) , Zien
and Ostrach (1970) ). Furthermore, it is important to note that
time-varying shape of the tube walls during peristalsis which is a
realistic assumption due to strict time dependency of the problem
cannot be satisﬁed using periodicity since periodicity makes any
peristaltic pumping shape invariant under translation ( Walker
and Shelley, 2010 ). This issue is of more signiﬁcance when bolus
reaches near the end of the ureter. Because during drainage of the
bolus from the ureteral outlet, great temporal changes in bolus
shape and pressure as well as outlet ﬂow rate occurs.

the entrance distance effect to the ﬂow ﬁeld is negligible.
The foremost beneﬁt of this model is that ureteral wall non-
linear properties as well as a precise geometry of ureteral luminal
area during peristalsis have been considered together. Moreover,
the cell to cell wall stimulation which occurs in the physiological
conditions of ureteral peristaltic function was applied through an
external barrier (The rigid contact surface shown in Fig. 1 ). This
kind of stimulation results in time-varying shape of the moving
bolus during peristalsis. Furthermore, one of the major advan-
tages of the presented research is the assumed axisymmetric
model compared to the related numerous 2-D models studied
before ( Vahidi and Fatouraee, 2007 ; Teran et al., 2008 ; Jime´ nez-
Lozano, 2009 ; Walker and Shelley, 2010 ). Since biological tubes
have large nonlinearity in geometry and material properties, the
inevitable effect of dimension in ﬁnite element models in any
analysis is obvious. The model presented in this paper can be
considered as a signiﬁcant step simulating 3-D problems and has
many applications analyzing peristaltic ﬂow in collapsible tracts
of different mammalian organs.
Various ﬂow patterns in the ureter during peristalsis were
reported that are isolated-bolus, boluses in contact, leaky-bolus
and open-tube ﬂow ( Grifﬁths, 1987 , 1989; Ohlson, 1989a ,b). The
model presented in this paper describes the condition in which
the common peristaltic mechanism in human ureteral function
(isolated bolus transportation) exists ( Ohlson, 1989b ).
The urine pressure proﬁle achieved in the ureter during
peristalsis in the presented numerical model ( Fig. 3 ) has a good
agreement with Weinberg’s observations ( Weinberg, 1974 ). He
reported that the maximum ureteral luminal pressure during
peristalsis permanently occurs behind the moving bolus.
The ﬂow rate results achieved in this study (with the max-
imum and average ﬂow rates of 0.917 ml/min and 0.567 ml/min,
respectively) as shown in Table 1 , are in accordance with the
ﬁndings of previously published theoretical ( Grifﬁths, 1989 ) and
clinical ( Ohlson, 1989b ) investigations. In particular, through
urography of eight urinary systems (that were chosen for mor-
phometry and subsequent volumetry), Ohlson (1989b) reported
that volumetric ﬂow rate of a human isolated urine bolus would
be between 0.265 ml/min and 2.45 ml/min. Furthermore, Grifﬁths
(1989) reported that the corresponding ﬂow rate of isolated bolus
(the intrinsic upper limit to the ﬂow rate that can be transported
by steady peristalsis) is probably of order 2 ml/min. However, he
also referred that there are physiologically high urine pressures
and ﬂow rates which are possibly attained only occasionally and
for short periods.
The maximum magnitude of bolus ﬂow rate of the presented
numerical model was slightly smaller than the maximum magni-
tudes reported from previous studies referred above. This differ-
ence may be due to the fact that the ureteropelvic valve
mechanism in the ureter was ignored in the presented model,
leading to the ureteropelvic reﬂux. The ignorance was done to
quantify the anti-reﬂux effect of the physiological function of this
valve. This gives us a worthwhile understanding of the function of
this valve. The valve mechanism there is so that does not allow
urine backﬂow to the kidney. In other words, the simple papillae
which drain the single reniculus do not permit this backﬂow due
to their convexity as well as the slit-like opening of the collecting
ducts into the papillae ( Ransley and Risdon, 1975 ). Dysfunction of
this mechanism may lead to reﬂux nephropathy that most often
occurs in cases of pyelorenal backﬂow with infected urine
(Jorgensen, 1986 ).
The results marked a signiﬁcant role of ﬂuid–structure inter-
action on ﬂow analysis within a uniform compliant tube experi-
encing peristalsis. Similar to other engineering models that
simulate a system as intricate as a living organism, we made
several simpliﬁcations in our simulation. These simpliﬁcations
were mainly in (1) geometry; in reality, the ureteral collapsed
lumen is stellate in form ( Woodburne and Lapides, 1972 ;
Jime´ nez-Lozano, 2009 ), (2) rate of peristalsis; the contraction
wave velocity may vary during peristalsis through the ureter
due to the controlling function of a pacemaker in the renal
pelvis ( Constantinou, 1974 ; Zawalinski et al., 1975 ; Gosling and
Constantinou, 1976) and (3) ureteral wall loading conditions; the
conditions relate to the pre-stresses in the ureter that strengthens
it to resist urine luminal forces ( Hansen and Gregersen, 1999 ). It
is worth to note that the total stress (i.e., passive plus active)–
strain curve from Yin and Fung (1971) was used to present the
mechanical properties of the ureteral wall during peristalsis. In
this work, a rigid moving contact surface applies an external load
on the ureteral wall. This mechanical longitudinal stimulation
makes the ureter collapsed in whole length, from behind of the
moving bolus up to the ureteral inlet. Although this assumption
simpliﬁes the ureteral wall properties from physiological point of
view, it seems more appropriate than implementing the passive
stress–strain curve due to referred characteristics of the wall
stimulation in our study.
As yet, there are numerous unanswered questions in this
research ﬁeld that can be answered using the approach imple-
mented in this study. For instance, the knotty nature of reﬂux and
order of effects of different mechanical and biological parameters
are not well understood. The direct interaction between the two
components of the urinary system (kidney and bladder) has
barely acknowledged any theoretical insight. In addition, the wall
activation by a pacemaker that makes peristalsis and the detailed
progression of the elastic deformation in the ureteral muscle
which is highly nonlinear has not been completely understood.
There exist some great difﬁculties in solving such complicated
FSI problems due to the restrictions in computational tools and
costs. We thereby hope that through developments in numerical
methods as well as further studies about the sophisticated nature
of ureteral valve’s non-return action, valve mechanisms of the
ureteropelvic and ureterovesical junctions can be simulated and
better understood through a 3-D analysis by the presented
approach. The higher ﬂow rate mechanisms of leaky-bolus ﬂow

5. Conclusion
In this research, a biomechanical analysis was performed to
improve our understanding of the phenomenon of urine peristal-
tic transport from the kidney to the bladder. In-vivo morpho-
metric data of ureteral lumen during peristalsis were used to
construct a model which simulates ureteral peristalsis imple-
menting mechanical wall stimulation through contact analysis.
Results showed that urine reﬂux to the kidney occur by the wave
onset and the quantity of ureteropelvic reﬂux decreases by
distally propagating the wave through the ureter. Furthermore,
quantitative analysis of urine ﬂow rate at the ureteral outlet
during peristalsis was performed. This study quantitatively
revealed the vital anti-reﬂux function of ureteropelvic junction.
The numerical results may be helpful for experts in the ﬁeld of
experimental biomedicine to design more realistic experiments
which lead to better understanding of the intricate nature of
ureteral peristalsis.
